Dice on the ball: Mahaha vs Slam Union

The best movement-only sack route at the start of every defending turn, followed by the tactical verdict. Turns where the active team holds the ball are omitted.

Method and limitations

The search allows up to two setup movers to provide assists, cancel defensive assists or open hitter lanes, and includes basic Frenzy second blocks and crowd-surfs. Percentages use the rerolls actually banked at the start of the turn. “Ball down” means the ball is dislodged without a turnover. Preparatory blocks, Dump-Off and recovery after a spill are assessed separately.

DE T3

Best route: the Dodge Assassin first marks out the remaining defensive assist. The Dark Elf Wrestle lineman then takes a 3+ dodge, a 2+ dodge and two rushes into a one-die blitz. With three rerolls, all-in gives 60.9% ball down and 25.3% turnover; holding one gives 57.9% and 27.1%. Wrestle makes both-down a successful sack without a turnover, which is why this beats the Block Witch Elf line despite the approach rolls.

The ordinary lineman Jawa actually blitzed with can also reach a one-die hit after the same assist cancellation, with only a 2+ dodge and a rushed block. Under the calculator's normal policy, which rerolls an initial both-down, that line is 52.5% ball down and 26.5% turnover all-in. Counting a final both-down as an acceptable turnover sack raises its total spill chance to about 63.0%. If Jawa accepts the first both-down instead of rerolling it, the split is about 47.3% clean sack, 23.6% both-down sack and 37.0% total turnover, or 70.9% ball spilled by either route.

Verdict: slight preference for the Wrestle blitz, holding one reroll. It gives 57.9% clean ball down and 27.1% turnover, against 52.1% and 26.7% for Jawa's ordinary-lineman route under the same policy. The 5.8-point clean-sack edge is useful but not decisive. Jawa's shorter line is entirely defensible, and becomes the higher pure spill chance if a both-down turnover is acceptable. With four standing Union players beside the carrier, however, a turnover-spill is much less valuable than a live turn with reroll cover.

DE T4

Best route: Dodge Blitzer #6 jumps a prone body on 4+, makes 3+ and 2+ dodges, then rushes the one-die block. With two rerolls, all-in gives 46.2% ball down and 42.6% turnover; holding one gives 38.3% and 47.8%. The extra 8.0 points of all-in sacks are exactly the branches that spend the final reroll, so every one of them reaches the loose ball with no reroll cover.

Recovery changes the judgment. On a push knockdown, push the Thrower to A1/W1 and follow to A2/W1. That keeps the ball in bounds and puts the hitter beside the likely bounces. Under all-in, a successful sack leaves two rerolls 37.5% of the time, one 45.2%, and none 17.3%. Holding one removes that last branch: successful sacks leave two rerolls 45.4% of the time and one 54.6%.

The Leader Runner is the primary recovery player. Depending on the bounce, the route plus pickup is roughly 46% to 63%; a bounce onto the hitter instead offers about a one-in-eight immediate catch chance, normally on 2+. The prone Assassin can stand and attack A2/W2 as the main backup, at roughly 51% conditional on the sack. The prone lineman can stand and reach the A4 depth line about 84% of the time to screen or chase a short bounce. Dodge Blitzer #5 can reach A5 reliably, A4 at about 72%, and A3 at about 55%, but cannot recover a ball left from A2 through the Away end zone this turn. The rest of Mahaha are too far away to be meaningful immediate recoverers.

These are directional recovery estimates rather than calculator outputs, but together they put immediate Mahaha possession at roughly 60% after a successful sack, or about 28% from the start of the turn. Moving the Runner in as a pre-hit assist lowers the all-in sack to 41.4% ball down and 53.9% turnover; using Dodge Blitzer #5 as the assist is worse at 33.0% and 63.8%.

Verdict: take the unsupported Dodge Blitzer #6 sack, holding one reroll, then recover. Do not spend either recovery player before the hit. This gives up the zero-reroll success branches to guarantee pickup and escape cover whenever the ball comes down.

The route Dimitri actually took first used the Union lineman's two-dice block to clear the Witch Elf, then moved two linemen, the Wrestle lineman, the Diving Tackle Blitzer and the Leader Thrower to open a corridor. From the prepared board at cmd:2635, the no-setup Frenzy Blitzer route actually taken gives 93.1% ball down and 6.4% turnover with two rerolls left. Accounting for whether the opening block succeeds immediately, consumes the Leader reroll or fails twice, the full actual plan is about 92.2% ball down and 7.5% turnover before pricing Dump-Off.

The corrected search finds a stronger sack on that same prepared board: move one Catcher to H7/W3 before the blitz. The Catcher supplies a second assist and vacates the shorter Frenzy lane, raising the prepared hit to 95.9% ball down and 3.7% turnover with two rerolls. Including the opening block gives roughly 94.9% ball down and 4.8% turnover. That is the highest sack chance, but it spends the Catcher who performed the actual pickup, so it cannot inherit the same recovery-and-handoff percentage.

This preparation mattered beyond the sack. The Thrower's move vacated the recovery lane, one Catcher could collect a favourable westward scatter, and the other remained available for the handoff and scoring run. On the actual H8/W2 loose-ball square, the pickup, rush, Catch-covered handoff, Dodge-covered exit and two scoring rushes are about 83.6% with two team rerolls, 76.0% with one, and 45.6% with none. Dump-Off can instead scatter to eight different squares, so there is no honest single touchdown percentage for the full line; several scatters are recoverable, several land on Union players who have already acted, and H9/W4 favours Mahaha.

There was a stronger risk-adjusted hybrid. After the opening block and the first two corridor moves, a Catcher can move from H11/W3 to H7/W3 as the assist. That also vacates a clean lane for the Wrestle lineman's two-dice blitz from H9/W2. From the prepared board it gives 93.8% ball down and 0.7% turnover. Folding in the opening block produces about 92.6% ball down and 1.9% turnover. It spends one Catcher before the spill, but leaves the other Catcher and the scoring corridor available.

Mid-turn results still need activation-state checking. At the later prepared snapshot the search can print nominal extra moves by players who have already acted; those are illegal and must be discarded. The Catcher-assisted alternatives above use an unactivated Catcher and are legal. The no-setup 93.1% Frenzy line remains the route Dimitri actually took.

Verdict: take the prepared Wrestle hybrid. The actual Frenzy plan was a coherent win-now route and its extra preparation is why a sack could become a recovery and touchdown, something the immediate start-of-turn calculation misses. Against the Catcher-assisted Frenzy alternative, the hybrid gives up about 2.3 points of full-plan sack chance to cut turnover by about 2.9 points, keeps Wrestle as the clean both-down answer, and retains one Catcher plus the scoring lane. At 1-0 up, that is the better balance. The actual line remains defensible because preserving both Catchers created the pickup-and-handoff route that produced 2-0.
